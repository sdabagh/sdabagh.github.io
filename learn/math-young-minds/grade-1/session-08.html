📋 Session plan (for teachers)

Session 8 — 2D & 3D shapes: building and breaking apart

Grade 1 · Math for Young Minds Total time: ~18 minutes Common Core: 1.G.A.2 Today's idea: Shapes can be built from smaller shapes. And 3D shapes have flat faces that are 2D shapes.

What students will be able to do

By the end of this session, the student can:

  • Put two triangles together to make a new 2D shape.
  • Name a cube, sphere, and cylinder when they see one.
  • Count the flat faces on a cube (6).

Materials

  • Pattern blocks (or paper cut-outs of triangles, squares, rhombuses)
  • Small boxes, cans, or cubes for 3D shapes
  • Worksheet (one per student)
  • Pencil

Substitution: No pattern blocks? Cut triangles from paper. No cans? A tissue box (cube-ish), a ball, a soup can from the snack shelf all work.

New words

Word Meaning we use in class
face The flat side of a 3D shape.
cube A 3D shape with 6 square faces.
sphere A 3D shape like a ball — perfectly round.
cylinder A 3D shape like a soup can — round top and bottom, straight sides.

That's the entire vocabulary for today. No other terms.

Heads-up — common confusions

  • Circle vs. sphere. A circle is flat. A sphere is a ball — you can hold it. Show both side by side.
  • A cube has 6 faces, not 4. Kids see the front, back, left, right and stop. Remind them: "Count the top and the bottom too."
  • Calling everything round "a circle." A ball is a sphere. A can is a cylinder. Gently correct: "Yes, it's round — and it's a sphere."

Plan

1 · Hello & today's idea — 2 min

"Today we're shape builders. Small shapes can come together to make bigger shapes. And big 3D shapes — like boxes and cans — have flat sides too."

Hold up two triangles. Slide them together to make a square.

"Two triangles — one square. Shapes build shapes."

Then hold up a small box.

"This is a cube. Watch — it has flat sides called faces."

2 · Hands-on explore — 6 min

Give each pair some pattern blocks (or paper triangles) and a 3D object (box, can, or ball).

"First job: take two triangles. Put them together. What new shape did you make?"

Walk around. Watch for:

  • Students sliding edges together (not overlapping).
  • Students naming the new shape — a square, a rhombus, or a bigger triangle.

After about 2 minutes, switch:

"Now pick up your 3D shape. Touch each flat side. Say a number for each one. That's a face."

If a child has a cube, kneel beside them. Guide their finger to the top and bottom faces too.

"How many faces? Don't forget the bottom and the back."

3 · Connect to the math — 3 min

Stand at the front. Hold up two triangles and a cube.

"Two triangles make a..." Wait. "Square. Yes."

Hold up the cube. Count the faces slowly, turning it: "One, two, three, four, five, six. A cube has six faces. Each face is a square."

Hold up a ball.

"This is a sphere. No flat faces. All round."

Hold up a can.

"This is a cylinder. Two round faces — top and bottom. And one curved side."

4 · Practice with support — 5 min

Pass out the worksheet.

Problem 1 — together at the board:

"Put two triangles together. What 2D shape do you make?"

Do it on the board. Answer: a square (or a rhombus, depending on the triangles). Both are right.

Problem 2 — solo:

"How many flat faces does a cube have?" Answer: 6.

If a student writes 4, hand them a cube. "Count the bottom and the back too."

Problem 3 — solo:

"Find a cylinder in the room. What is it?"

Let them walk and point. A soup can, a coffee mug, a thermos — all good.

Problem 4 — stretch:

"Build a tower from 3 different 3D shapes. Draw it. Label each shape."

They can use cube, sphere, cylinder, or a cone if you have one. Labels matter more than neat drawings.

5 · What we did + Try at home — 2 min

"Today you built shapes from shapes. You found faces on a cube. You named a sphere and a cylinder."

Hold up the cube one more time. "How many faces?" Wait. "Six. You got it."

"Tonight, walk around your house. Find a cube, a sphere, and a cylinder. Draw each one or tell someone about it. A dice is a cube. An orange is a sphere. A paper towel roll is a cylinder."

Observation rubric — what to notice in this session

Use this during the session. One observation per student is plenty.

Where the student is What you'd see
Developing Mixes up circle and sphere. Counts only 4 faces on a cube. Needs the shape in their hands to name it.
Using Combines two triangles into a new shape. Names cube, sphere, cylinder. Counts 6 faces on a cube.
Extending Notices that a cylinder's top is a circle. Builds new shapes from 3+ pieces. Spots cubes and cylinders around the room without prompting.

No fail state. "Developing" today is "using" next week.

What's next

You've finished Grade 1. Together this year you've done adding, subtracting, place value, comparing, measuring, time, and shapes. That's a whole math toolkit. Next stop: Grade 2.

🏠 Family guide (for parents)

Math for Young Minds · Grade 1 · Session 8

Tonight: hunt for shapes around the house

What your child did today

In class today, we explored 2D and 3D shapes.

We put pattern blocks together to make new shapes — two triangles can come together to make a square. Then we looked at a stack of small boxes and cans and noticed the flat faces on each one. A cube, for example, has 6 square faces — not 4. We're learning to count them all, including the bottom and the back.

Why this matters

Shapes are how kids start to see math in the real world. A can is a cylinder. A ball is a sphere. A dice is a cube. When a child notices that a 3D shape is built from flat 2D faces, they're starting to think like a geometer — taking things apart and putting them back together in their mind. That same habit shows up later in fractions, area, and even algebra. We're not in a rush. Understanding comes first; speed comes on its own.

🏠 Try this tonight (1 minute)

Walk through one room together. Find a cube, a sphere, and a cylinder. Draw or describe each one.

Easy finds
A dice (cube)
An orange (sphere)
A soda can (cylinder)
A Rubik's cube
A basketball
A paper towel roll

The script:

"Can you find me something shaped like a ball? What about a can? What about a little box with square sides?"

If they pick something up, ask:

"How many flat sides — faces — does it have?"

That's it. One minute, three shapes.

Words your child is learning

  • Face — the flat side of a 3D shape
  • Cube — a 3D shape with 6 square faces
  • Sphere — a 3D shape like a ball — perfectly round
  • Cylinder — a 3D shape like a soup can — round top and bottom, straight sides

If your child says…

"This is easy — it's just a circle."

Gently push a little. A circle is flat — you can draw it on paper. A ball isn't flat; it's a sphere. A can isn't a circle either; it's a cylinder. Ask: "Could you draw this on paper, or does it stick out?" That question alone teaches the 2D vs. 3D difference.

"A cube has 4 sides."

Very common. Hand them a small box and count together — top, bottom, front, back, left, right. That's 6 faces. The bottom and back are easy to forget because we can't see them. Slowing down to count each one is the whole point.

"I don't want to."

That's okay. Try again tomorrow at snack time — a cracker box, an apple, a cup. Math at this age is just noticing the shapes already around us.
