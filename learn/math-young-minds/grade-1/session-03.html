Session 3 вҖ” Place value: tens and ones

Grade 1 В· Math for Young Minds Total time: ~19 minutes Common Core: 1.NBT.B.2 Today's idea: A 2-digit number is made of tens and ones. The left digit is tens. The right digit is ones.

What students will be able to do

By the end of this session, the student can:

  • Show a 2-digit number as a group of tens and a group of ones.
  • Write the number when shown a tens + ones picture (3 tens + 5 ones = 35).
  • Read 2-digit numbers correctly.

Materials

  • 10вҖ“20 snap cubes per student (or pair)
  • A place-value mat with tens and ones columns
  • Worksheet (one per student)
  • Pencil

Substitution: No cubes? Use bundles of 10 sticks tied with a rubber band, or a dime (1 ten) and pennies (ones). Anything that lets kids see a group of 10 as one bundle works.

New words

Word Meaning we use in class
tens The digit that tells how many groups of 10 there are.
ones The digit that tells how many single units there are.

That's the entire vocabulary for today. No other terms.

Heads-up вҖ” common confusions

  • "Twenty-three" written as 203. The child hears "twenty" and writes 20, then tacks on 3. Slow them down. "23 has 2 tens and 3 ones. Just two digits."
  • Mixing up 13 and 30. Thirteen is 1 ten and 3 ones. Thirty is 3 tens and 0 ones. Say them slowly, side by side.
  • Forgetting which side is which. Tens on the left, ones on the right. Point at the mat every time.

Plan

1 В· Hello & today's idea вҖ” 2 min

Hold up one stack of 10 snap cubes. Then hold up 3 single cubes.

"This stack is one ten. These three are ones. Together, that's thirteen вҖ” 1 ten and 3 ones."

Write 13 on the board. Point at the 1. "Tens." Point at the 3. "Ones."

"Today, every number we say will have tens and ones."

2 В· Hands-on explore вҖ” 6 min

Give each student cubes and a place-value mat.

"Build me 34. Snap 10 cubes together. Make another ten. Make another ten. That's 3 tens. Then put 4 single cubes on the ones side."

Walk around. Watch for:

  • Tens stacks on the left, ones on the right.
  • Counting bundles as "10, 20, 30" вҖ” not "1, 2, 3."
  • Tens that actually have 10 cubes (not 9, not 11).

When most students have 34 built:

"How many tens?" (3) "How many ones?" (4) "So the number isвҖҰ" (34)

If a student writes 304, kneel down. "Just two digits. 3 tens, 4 ones. 34."

3 В· Connect to the math вҖ” 3 min

At the board, draw two columns: tens | ones.

Write 3 under tens, 4 under ones.

"Three tens. Four ones. That's thirty-four. The left digit tells tens. The right digit tells ones."

Now write 43.

"Same digits! But now it's 4 tens and 3 ones. Forty-three. Position matters."

Point to the mat. "Tens on the left. Ones on the right. Always."

4 В· Practice with support вҖ” 6 min

Pass out the worksheet. Do problem 1 together. Then let students try the rest.

Problem 1 (together): Show 34 with bundles. How many tens? How many ones? вҶ’ 3 tens and 4 ones.

Problem 2 (solo): How many ones are in 7 tens? вҶ’ 70. (Each ten is 10 ones. 7 tens is 70 ones.)

Problem 3 (solo): Write the number that has 5 tens and 2 ones. вҶ’ 52.

Problem 4 (stretch): What's the biggest 2-digit number you can make? The smallest? вҶ’ Biggest: 99 (9 tens, 9 ones). Smallest: 10 (1 ten, 0 ones).

If a child is stuck, hand them cubes. Build it together.

5 В· What we did + Try at home вҖ” 2 min

"Today you learned that a 2-digit number is made of tens and ones. Left digit, tens. Right digit, ones."

Write 52 on the board. "How many tens?" (5) "How many ones?" (2)

"Tonight, find a 2-digit number around your house. A house number on a door. A page number in a book. The minutes on a clock. Say its tens and its ones out loud."

Observation rubric вҖ” what to notice in this session

Use this during the session, not as a test. One observation per student is plenty.

Where the student is What you'd see
Developing Mixes up the columns, or writes 23 as "203". May need to count each cube in a ten. Needs the mat and your finger.
Using Builds a 2-digit number with the right number of tens and ones. Reads it correctly. Writes the digits in the right spots.
Extending Sees a number like 47 and instantly says "4 tens, 7 ones" without cubes. Can talk about the biggest or smallest 2-digit number.

No fail state. "Developing" today is "using" next week.

What's next (Session 4)

In Session 4 вҖ” Adding within 20, we use ten-frames to add bigger sums (like 8 + 5) by making 10 first. Today's idea вҖ” that 10 is a special group вҖ” comes back strong.

рҹҸ  Family guide (for parents)

Math for Young Minds В· Grade 1 В· Session 3

Tonight: find a 2-digit number around the house

What your child did today

In class today, we worked on place value вҖ” the idea that where a digit sits inside a number changes what it means.

We used bundles of 10 (snap cubes, grouped in tens) to build numbers up to 100. We practiced reading numbers like 34 as "3 tens and 4 ones."

The big idea: the digit on the left tells you how many tens. The digit on the right tells you how many ones.

So 52 isn't just "5 and 2." It's 5 tens (that's 50) and 2 ones вҖ” fifty-two.

Why this matters

This is the moment numbers stop being a list to recite and start being built out of parts.

Once your child sees that 34 is really 30 + 4, a whole lot of math gets easier later: adding bigger numbers, subtracting, even money. It's the difference between memorizing "thirty-four" and understanding what thirty-four is made of.

We're not in a rush. Understanding comes before speed.

If your child saysвҖҰ

"This is easy вҖ” I already know my numbers."

Wonderful. Try a tricky pair: ask them the difference between 13 and 30. Both have a "3" and a "1" or a "0" вҖ” but the position changes everything. If they can explain that, they really get it.

"Wait вҖ” is twenty-three written 203?"

This is one of the most common mix-ups, and it's a sign their brain is working hard. Grab 2 small groups of 10 things and 3 extras. Show them: 2 tens and 3 ones is 23, not 203. The position does the work вҖ” no extra zero needed.

"I don't want to."

That's okay. Try again tomorrow with a number they care about вҖ” their age plus one, the page they're on in a story, the channel number on the TV. Math at this age should feel like noticing, not working.

What's next

In our next session, we'll start adding within 20. We'll use ten-frames to add bigger sums like 8 + 5 by "making 10" first. Today's tens-and-ones thinking is exactly what makes that click.

Thanks for taking a minute tonight. These small moments are where math lives.
