📋 Session plan (for teachers)

Session 5 — Fractions as numbers (on a number line)

Grade 3 · Math for Young Minds Total time: ~22 minutes Common Core: 3.NF.A.2 Today's idea: A fraction is a number. It has its own spot on the number line.

What students will be able to do

By the end of this session, the student can:

  • Place 1/2, 1/3, and 1/4 on a number line from 0 to 1.
  • Read fractions out loud: one-half, one-third, one-fourth.
  • Explain why 1/2 is bigger than 1/3 — bigger bottom number means smaller pieces.

Materials

  • A piece of string stretched between two desks (the "live" number line)
  • Paper strips ~25 cm long (one per pair)
  • Rulers (or just fold the strips)
  • Worksheet (one per student)
  • Pencils
  • Scissors (optional)

Substitution: No string? Draw a long line on the board with 0 on the left and 1 on the right. Any long, straight thing works — a ribbon, a piece of yarn, even a meter stick.

New words

Word Meaning we use in class
fraction A number that is part of a whole.
numerator The top number — how many parts we have.
denominator The bottom number — how many equal parts the whole is split into.
number line A straight line where every number has its own spot.

Heads-up — common confusions

  • Many kids will say 1/3 is bigger than 1/2 because 3 is more than 2. It's the opposite — bigger denominator means smaller pieces. The folded strip will show this.
  • Mixing up the numerator (top) and denominator (bottom). Point to top and bottom every time you say the word.
  • Thinking fractions are only "pizza slices." Today we show fractions live on a number line — they are real numbers.
  • Comparing fractions by just reading the numbers, not by looking at the size of the parts.

Plan

1 · Hello & today's idea — 2 min

Point to the string stretched between two desks. Tape a card with 0 on the left end and 1 on the right end.

"This string is a number line. On the left is 0. On the right is 1. Today we're going to find numbers that live between 0 and 1."

Ask: "Is there any number between 0 and 1?"

Take a few answers. Don't correct yet.

"There is. They're called fractions. A fraction is a number that is part of a whole."

2 · Hands-on explore — 7 min

Give each pair a paper strip ~25 cm long.

"Imagine your strip is the number line from 0 to 1. The left end is 0. The right end is 1."

Problem 1 — together: "Fold your strip into 2 equal parts. Open it back up. Make a mark on the fold. That mark is 1/2."

Walk around. Help with folds. Read it out loud together: "one-half."

Ask: "Where does 1/2 land?"

Listen for: "In the middle." / "Halfway." That's the answer.

Now hang one student's strip along the string. The 1/2 mark sits right in the middle.

"1/2 is a number. It has its own spot — right in the middle of 0 and 1."

3 · Connect to the math — 4 min

Write on the board:

        1   ← numerator (how many parts we have)
       ───
        2   ← denominator (how many equal parts in the whole)

Point to the top: "Numerator. How many parts we have." Point to the bottom: "Denominator. How many equal parts the whole is split into."

Read it out loud together: "one-half."

Now write next to it:

   1/2     1/3     1/4
  half    third   fourth

"The bigger the denominator, the more pieces the whole is cut into. More pieces means each piece is smaller."

Say it again with hands: hold hands wide apart for halves, closer for thirds, even closer for fourths.

4 · Practice with support — 7 min

Pass out the worksheet. Students keep their strips.

Problem 2 (solo): "Take a new strip. Fold it into 4 equal parts. Mark 1/4, 2/4, 3/4. Now — where does 1/2 land on this strip?"

Answer: 1/2 = 2/4 — same spot! Big deal. Let them notice it.

Problem 3 (solo): "Take a new strip. Fold it into 3 equal parts. Mark 1/3 and 2/3. Which is bigger: 1/3 or 1/4?"

Many will predict 1/4 because 4 > 3. Have them lay the two strips side by side. 1/3 is bigger. Bigger denominator, smaller piece.

Problem 4 (stretch): "Draw a number line from 0 to 1. Mark 1/2, 1/3, 1/4 in different colors. Which fraction is closest to 0? Which is closest to 1/2?"

Answer: 1/4 is closest to 0. 1/3 is between 1/4 and 1/2.

Circulate. If a student is stuck, hand them their folded strips and let them line up the marks.

5 · What we did + Try at home — 2 min

"Today you learned that a fraction is a number. It has its own spot on the number line. And a bigger bottom number means smaller pieces."

Send the take-home note:

"Tonight, find something at home you can fold into halves or fourths — a napkin, a piece of bread, a paper plate. Fold it. Look at the parts. Talk about which is bigger: 1/2 or 1/4 of the same thing."

Observation rubric — what to notice in this session

Use this during the session, not as a test. One observation per student is plenty.

Where the student is What you'd see
Developing Still thinks 1/3 is bigger than 1/2 because 3 > 2. Mixes up numerator and denominator. Needs the folded strip to find the mark.
Using Folds the strip into equal parts. Places 1/2, 1/3, 1/4 on the number line. Reads them out loud correctly. Sees that 1/2 is bigger than 1/3.
Extending Notices that 1/2 = 2/4 — same spot. Can order 1/4, 1/3, 1/2 from smallest to biggest without the strip.

No fail state. "Developing" today is "using" next week.

What's next (Session 6)

Building on this, Session 6 — Equivalent fractions picks up the surprise from today: 1/2 = 2/4. Next time, we find many more pairs like that — fractions that look different but live at the same spot on the number line.

🏠 Family guide (for parents)

Math for Young Minds · Grade 3 · Session 5

A note for grown-ups: today we put fractions on a number line

What your child did today

In class today, we learned that a fraction is a number — not just a slice of pizza.

The big idea: every fraction has its own spot on the number line, just like 1, 2, or 3 do.

We stretched a piece of string between two desks and marked the spots for 1/2, 1/3, and 1/4. Then we folded paper strips to find those same spots between 0 and 1. Your child read them aloud: one-half, one-third, one-fourth.

Along the way, we discovered something surprising: 1/2 is bigger than 1/3, even though 3 is bigger than 2. The bigger the bottom number, the smaller the piece.

Why this matters

Fractions trip a lot of kids up because they look like two numbers stacked together, not one number. Putting them on a number line — between 0 and 1 — helps your child see that 1/2 is a real number with a real home. That foundation makes the rest of fraction work (adding, comparing, simplifying) feel solid instead of slippery. We're not in a rush. Understanding first.

🏠 Try this tonight (1 minute)

Find something at home you can fold into halves or fourths. A napkin, a piece of paper, a sandwich, a paper plate.

Fold it once → that's halves. Fold it again → that's fourths. Look at the pieces together.

Then ask:

  • "Which is bigger — 1/2 of this, or 1/4 of this?"
  • "Why do you think that is?"

Easy things to fold or cut:

Thing Fold into
A napkin fourths
A piece of bread halves, then quarters
A paper plate halves
An apple fourths (each piece is 1/4)
A sheet of paper halves or fourths

If your child says "1/4 is bigger because 4 is more" — don't correct them with words. Just point at the pieces. The eyes will do the teaching.

Words your child is learning

  • Fraction — a number that is part of a whole
  • Numerator — the top number; how many parts we have
  • Denominator — the bottom number; how many equal parts the whole is split into
  • Number line — a straight line where every number has its own spot

If your child says…

"This is easy." Great. Ask them to draw a number line from 0 to 1 and place 1/2, 1/3, and 1/4 on it. Then ask which one is closest to 0.

"This is hard." Also great. Grab a strip of paper and fold it together. Fold once for halves. Fold again for fourths. Let them touch the parts. Hands first, numbers later.

"I don't want to." That's okay. Skip the math words and just fold a napkin together at dinner. Ask which piece they'd rather have — the half or the quarter. That's the whole lesson, no worksheet needed.

What's next

Today your child noticed that 1/2 and 2/4 land on the same spot. Next session, we chase that idea — finding many more pairs of fractions that are secretly the same number. It's called equivalent fractions.

Thanks for folding a napkin with them tonight. These small kitchen-table moments are where math lives.
