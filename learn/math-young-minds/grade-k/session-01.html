Session 1 вҖ” Counting things I see

Kindergarten В· Math for Young Minds Total time: ~17 minutes Common Core: K.CC.A.1, K.CC.B.4, K.CC.B.5 Today's idea: Each thing gets one number. The last number you say is how many.

What students will be able to do

By the end of this session, the student can:

  • Point at things one at a time while saying number names in order.
  • Say how many things are in a group (1вҖ“10).
  • Notice that the last number they say is the answer.

Materials

  • A box of crayons for each pair of students (4вҖ“10 crayons per box is fine)
  • A small basket or paper plate (to hold the crayons)
  • Worksheet (one per student)
  • Pencil or crayon for marking

Substitution: No crayons? Use anything you have 4вҖ“10 of: paper clips, pasta pieces, beans, blocks, buttons. Avoid tiny or sharp things.

New words

Word Meaning we use in class
count To point at things one by one and say a number for each.
how many The total вҖ” the last number we say.

That's the entire vocabulary for today. No other terms.

Heads-up вҖ” common confusions

  • Skipping or doubling. A child may point at a crayon and say "two" for it. Gently slow down. "Let's go again вҖ” one crayon, one number."
  • Counting in the air. Some kids will say numbers without pointing. Slow them down by guiding their finger.
  • Memorizing without understanding. A child who can recite "1вҖ“2вҖ“3вҖ“4вҖ“5" might still not know what 5 means. The goal today is pointing, not reciting.
  • "How many?" A child may recount everything when asked. That's fine and developmentally normal. We're building the idea that the last number is the answer.

Plan

1 В· Hello & today's idea вҖ” 2 min

"Today we're going to count things together. Counting is when we look at something and say a number for it."

Hold up 3 crayons. Slowly count them out loud: "One. Two. Three. Three crayons."

Pause on that last word. Hold up your three fingers too.

2 В· Hands-on explore вҖ” 5 min

Hand each pair of students a small basket of crayons (4вҖ“10).

"Spread your crayons out on the table. Touch each one. Say a number for each one. Like this вҖ” one, two, three."

Walk around. Watch for:

  • Pointing or touching as they say each number.
  • Stopping when they've touched all the crayons.
  • Saying "how many" in a way that means the total.

If you see a child reciting "1вҖ“2вҖ“3вҖ“4вҖ“5" without pointing, kneel beside them and gently guide their finger to each crayon.

After ~3 minutes, ask one pair:

"How many crayons does your group have?"

Listen for whether they say the last number, or recount.

3 В· Connect to the math вҖ” 3 min

Stand at the front. Show 5 crayons on a plate.

"Let's count these together. Point with me."

Count slowly, pointing: one, two, three, four, five. Pause.

"How many?" Wait. Let them say it. "Five. The last number is how many."

Repeat with a different group of crayons (try 7).

"Here's the secret of counting: the last number you say is how many there are."

4 В· Practice with support вҖ” 5 min

Pass out the worksheet. Stand near students who need extra help.

"On your paper, you'll see groups of things. Touch each one. Say a number for each. The last number is how many. Then circle the right number."

Do problem 1 together at the board. Then let students try problems 2вҖ“4 with a neighbor.

If a child is stuck, slow them down. Point with their finger.

5 В· What we did + Try at home вҖ” 2 min

"Today you learned how to count. Each thing gets one number. The last number is how many."

Hold up 4 fingers. "How many?" Wait. "Four. You did it."

Wave the family guide.

"Take this home. Tonight, count something with someone in your family. It can be anything вҖ” your shoes, your toys, the steps in your house."

Observation rubric вҖ” what to notice in this session

Use this during the session, not as a test. One observation per student is plenty.

Where the student is What you'd see
Developing May skip items, double-count, or say numbers without pointing. Needs your hand to guide.
Using Points at each item once, says each number once, can answer "how many?" with the last number.
Extending Recognizes a small group's size without counting ("That's 3!"). Or starts counting groups of 10+.

No fail state. "Developing" today is "using" next week.

What's next (Session 2)

In Session 2 вҖ” Counting bigger groups (10вҖ“20), we keep the same pointing-and-counting habit but with bigger numbers вҖ” windows in the room, steps on a stairway, friends in the class.

рҹҸ  Family guide (for parents)

Math for Young Minds В· Kindergarten В· Session 1

Tonight: count something together

What your child did today

In class today, we practiced counting.

The big idea: when you count, each thing gets one number, and the last number you say is how many there are.

We counted crayons, fingers, and a couple of small groups together.

This sounds simple. It's actually one of the most important math skills your child will ever learn.

Why this matters

A 5-year-old can often recite "1, 2, 3, 4, 5" before they really understand what counting means.

Real counting is two ideas working together:

  1. Each thing gets one number (you can't skip or double-count).
  2. The last number you say is the total (you don't need to count again to find out "how many").

These two ideas вҖ” called one-to-one correspondence and cardinality if you ever want to look them up вҖ” are the foundation for everything else: addition, subtraction, place value, multiplication, everything.

We are not in a rush. The goal is understanding, not speed.

рҹҸ  Try this tonight (1 minute)

Pick something you have. Anything in groups of 3 to 10 works.

Easy starters
Shoes by the door
Steps from the couch to the kitchen
Spoons in a drawer
Toys in a basket

The script:

"Let's count these together. Touch each one. Say a number for each."

Help them point at each thing as they say the number. Move slowly.

When they finish, ask:

"How many?"

If they recount, that's fine вҖ” totally normal at this age. Over time, they'll start trusting that the last number IS the answer, and they won't need to recount.

Words your child is learning

  • Count вҖ” point at things one by one and say a number for each
  • How many вҖ” the total вҖ” the last number we say

That's the whole list for today.

If your child saysвҖҰ

"I already know how to count."

Wonderful. Ask them to count something a bit bigger вҖ” say 8 or 10 things. Watch whether they slow down, point at each one, and say each number clearly. Speed isn't the win here. Accuracy is.

"That's too easy."

Great. Try a quick game: hide some of the things under a cloth or your hand. "How many do you think are under here?" (Don't peek.) Then count to check. They're learning that they can estimate, then verify.

"I don't want to."

That's okay. Try again tomorrow with something they actually like вҖ” Lego pieces, snacks, stuffed animals. Math at this age should feel like noticing the world, not doing work.

What's next

In our next session, we'll practice the same skill with bigger numbers вҖ” windows, steps, classmates. Same idea, just more practice.

Thanks for taking a minute tonight. These small kitchen-table moments are where math lives.
