Session 2 вҖ” Counting bigger groups (10вҖ“20)

Kindergarten В· Math for Young Minds Total time: ~17 minutes Common Core: K.CC.A.2, K.CC.A.3 Today's idea: We can keep counting past ten вҖ” eleven, twelve, all the way to twenty.

What students will be able to do

By the end of this session, the student can:

  • Count things in a group all the way up to 20.
  • Keep pointing as the numbers get bigger.
  • Say the right number after ten вҖ” eleven, twelve, thirteenвҖҰ

Materials

  • Worksheet (one per student)
  • Pencil or crayon for marking
  • The classroom itself вҖ” windows, stairs, classmates вҖ” things to count

Substitution: If your room has fewer than 10 windows, count chairs, ceiling tiles, books on a shelf, or fingers and toes in a small group. Anything you have 10вҖ“20 of works.

New words

Word Meaning we use in class
bigger numbers Numbers past 10, all the way to 20.

That's the entire vocabulary for today. No other terms.

Heads-up вҖ” common confusions

  • "Ten-one, ten-two." After ten, some kids say "ten-one, ten-two" instead of eleven, twelve. That's normal. Gently say the right word together.
  • 13 and 30 sound alike. Same with 14 and 40. Slow down and stretch the word: "thir-teen."
  • Losing track partway. A child may stop and start over from 1. That's fine. Count to where they stopped, not from 1.

Plan

1 В· Hello & today's idea вҖ” 2 min

"Last time we counted up to ten. Today we're going bigger вҖ” all the way to twenty."

Hold up both hands. Count out loud, wiggling each finger: "One, two, threeвҖҰ nine, ten."

"Ten! But numbers don't stop there. Watch."

Keep going slowly: "E-leven, twelve, thirteen, fourteen, fifteenвҖҰ" up to twenty.

"Those are bigger numbers вҖ” past ten, all the way to twenty."

2 В· Hands-on explore вҖ” 5 min

Stand up. Point at the windows.

"Let's count the windows together. Point with me."

Count slowly, pointing at each window: "One, two, threeвҖҰ" Stretch thir-teen, four-teen if you get there.

"How many windows? The last number is how many."

Now try classmates.

"Stand up. We'll count every friend. Touch the top of their head gently as we count."

Walk around the circle. Count out loud together, one head at a time.

Watch for:

  • Pointing as they say each number.
  • Saying eleven, twelve (not ten-one, ten-two).
  • Staying with the group instead of racing ahead.

If a child says "ten-one," smile and say "e-leven" together.

3 В· Connect to the math вҖ” 3 min

Come back to the rug or seats.

"Counting past ten works the same way. Each thing gets one number. Keep pointing. The last number is how many."

Hold up a worksheet. Show a row of 12 stars.

"Let's count these stars together. Point with me."

Count slowly: "One, two, threeвҖҰ ten, e-leven, twelve."

"Twelve stars. The last number is how many."

4 В· Practice with support вҖ” 5 min

Pass out the worksheet. Stand near students who need extra help.

"Touch each one. Say a number for each. The last number is how many."

  • Problem 1 (together): Count 12 stars. Point at each one. Do this one out loud as a class.
  • Problem 2 (on your own): Count 16 dots.
  • Problem 3 (on your own): Count 19 fish.
  • Stretch: Draw 14 hearts. Then count them to check.

If a child gets stuck, kneel beside them. Point with their finger. Say the tricky words slowly together: thir-teen, four-teen, fif-teen.

5 В· What we did + Try at home вҖ” 2 min

"Today you counted bigger numbers вҖ” all the way to twenty. You pointed at each one. The last number was how many."

Hold up ten fingers, then wiggle them again. "How many if I count them twice? Twenty!"

"Tonight, find a group at home with more than ten things. Stairs. Windows. Books on a shelf. Buttons on a remote. Spoons in the drawer. Touch each one. Say a number for each. The last number is how many."

Observation rubric вҖ” what to notice in this session

Use this during the session, not as a test. One observation per student is plenty.

Where the student is What you'd see
Developing Loses track after ten, or says "ten-one, ten-two." May stop pointing as numbers get bigger. Needs your hand to guide.
Using Counts past ten with the right words (eleven, twelve, thirteenвҖҰ). Keeps pointing. Answers "how many?" with the last number.
Extending Counts smoothly to 20 without help. May start noticing the pattern (sixteen, seventeen, eighteenвҖҰ) or count past 20.

No fail state. "Developing" today is "using" next week.

рҹҸ  Family guide (for parents)

Math for Young Minds В· Kindergarten В· Session 2

Tonight: count something bigger than ten

What your child did today

In class today, we practiced counting bigger groups вҖ” all the way up to 20.

We counted windows in the classroom. We counted stairs. We counted classmates.

The big idea is the same as before: each thing gets one number, and the last number you say is how many. We just kept going past ten.

Numbers after ten have their own special names вҖ” eleven, twelve, thirteen вҖ” and those take a little practice.

Why this matters

Counting past ten is a real jump. The words stop following a simple pattern, and the groups get big enough that kids have to slow down and keep track.

This is where pointing at each thing really matters. It's how the brain stays organized when the numbers get longer.

We're not in a rush. Understanding comes before speed. Speed shows up on its own later.

A few things you might hear, and what to do:

  • If they say "ten-one, ten-two" instead of eleven, twelve вҖ” that's normal. Just say the right word together and keep going.
  • If they mix up thirteen and thirty, or fourteen and forty вҖ” slow down and stretch the word: thir-TEEN.
  • If they lose track partway, no need to start from 1. Count from where you stopped.

If your child saysвҖҰ

"I already know this."

Good. Ask them to count a bigger group вҖ” 15 or 18 things. Watch whether they point at each one and say each number clearly. Accuracy is the win, not speed.

"This is hard."

That's okay. The teen numbers are genuinely tricky. Count along with them, slowly. Say the words together. Their understanding is growing вҖ” it just takes a few tries.

"I don't want to."

Fine. Try again tomorrow with something they care about вҖ” toys, snacks, steps to their bedroom. Counting at this age should feel like noticing things, not doing work.
