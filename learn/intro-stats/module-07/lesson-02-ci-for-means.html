Why use t instead of z?

In most real-world situations, we don't know the population standard deviation œÉ. We only have the sample standard deviation s. When we use s to estimate œÉ, we introduce extra uncertainty, so we use the t-distribution instead of the normal (z) distribution.

2. The t-Distribution

üìñ The t-Distribution

The t-distribution (Student's t-distribution) is similar to the standard normal distribution but has heavier tails. It's used when:

  • The population standard deviation (œÉ) is unknown
  • We use the sample standard deviation (s) as an estimate

Key properties of the t-distribution:

  • Symmetric and bell-shaped (like the normal distribution)
  • Centered at 0
  • Has heavier tails than the normal distribution (more area in the tails)
  • Shape depends on degrees of freedom (df)
  • As df increases, t-distribution approaches the standard normal distribution

Degrees of Freedom

df = n - 1

where n is the sample size

üìù Example 1: Finding Degrees of Freedom

a) A sample of n = 25 students: df = 25 - 1 = 24

b) A sample of n = 100 voters: df = 100 - 1 = 99

c) A sample of n = 10 measurements: df = 10 - 1 = 9

Finding Critical t-Values (t*)

The critical value t* depends on two things:

  1. Confidence level (90%, 95%, 99%, etc.)
  2. Degrees of freedom (df = n - 1)

3. Constructing Confidence Intervals for Means

Steps to Construct a CI for Œº:

  1. Check conditions:
    • Random sample
    • Population is approximately normal OR n ‚â• 30 (Central Limit Theorem)
    • If n < 30, check that data doesn't have strong skewness or outliers
  2. Calculate sample statistics: xÃÑ and s
  3. Determine df: df = n - 1
  4. Find critical value: Look up t* for desired confidence level and df
  5. Calculate margin of error: E = t* √ó (s/‚àön)
  6. Construct interval: xÃÑ ¬± E
  7. Interpret: State conclusion in context

Rule of thumb:

When in doubt, use t. The t-distribution is almost always correct for means when œÉ is unknown. As n increases, t approaches z anyway, so t is the safer choice.

‚úÖ Check Your Understanding

Question 1: A sample of n = 20 has xÃÑ = 50 and s = 8. What are the degrees of freedom?

Answer: df = n - 1 = 20 - 1 = 19

Question 2: Why do we use the t-distribution instead of z when œÉ is unknown?

Answer: Using s to estimate œÉ introduces extra uncertainty. The t-distribution has heavier tails to account for this additional uncertainty, giving more conservative (wider) intervals.

Question 3: For a 95% CI with df = 10, would t* be larger or smaller than z* = 1.96?

Answer: Larger. With df = 10, t* = 2.228, which is larger than z* = 1.96. This makes the interval wider to account for the smaller sample size and using s instead of œÉ.

Question 4: A 99% CI for Œº is (45, 55). What is the margin of error?

Answer: The margin of error is 5. The interval width is 55 - 45 = 10, and margin of error is half the width: 10/2 = 5. Alternatively, the center is 50, and E = 55 - 50 = 5.

Question 5: If you want a narrower CI but keep the same confidence level, what should you do?

Answer: Increase the sample size (n). Since margin of error = t* √ó (s/‚àön), increasing n decreases s/‚àön, which decreases the margin of error and makes the interval narrower.
