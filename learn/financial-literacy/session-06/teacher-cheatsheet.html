📚 Key Vocabulary
Unit Price — cost per standard unit (per oz, per item, per gram)
Value — what you actually GET for your money (quality, use, longevity)
Price — what you PAY (the number on the tag)
Impulse Buy — an unplanned purchase made on emotion, not reason
Consumer — someone who buys goods or services
Shukr (شكر) — gratitude; appreciating what you already have
🧠 ND-Friendly Teaching Tips
  • Unit pricing — always use concrete visual examples (two yogurt containers). Show the math on board.
  • "Best Buy" game — physical comparison sheets work better than mental math for many students.
  • Impulse buying — avoid shame. "Everyone does it. The skill is recognizing it."
  • 24-hour rule — frame as a practical tool, not a punishment. "It's not 'no' — it's 'not right now.'"
  • Shukr — connect to feelings: "Appreciating what you have actually makes people happier." (Science supports this!)
  • Regret journal — keep it light. A $2 snack counts. Don't press for big stories.
  • Post agenda early — "In 5 minutes we switch to the game."
💬 Discussion Questions + Teacher Notes
  • "Is a $1 snack always a better deal than a $3 snack?"
    → No — if $3 snack is bigger or better quality, unit price matters. This opens the lesson.
  • "Has anyone bought something cheap that broke quickly?"
    → Price vs. value. Sometimes paying more is the smarter long-term choice.
  • "What makes you want to buy something you didn't plan to?"
    → Colors, ads, peer pressure, hunger, emotions. All valid. No judgment.
  • "Does the 24-hour rule actually work?"
    → For big items: yes. For $2 snack: probably not worth it. Apply proportionally.
  • "What does Shukr have to do with spending?"
    → Gratitude = contentment = less desire for unnecessary things. Practical spiritual link.
  • "Is all impulse buying bad?"
    → Not necessarily! Spontaneous gift for a friend = impulse buy that might be meaningful. Nuance matters.
🎮 "Best Buy" Comparison Game Setup
Groups get a comparison sheet with 3 scenarios. Find the best deal — but define "best"!
Scenario 1 — Yogurt:
Brand A: 8 oz for $2.00 = $0.25/oz
Brand B: 12 oz for $2.40 = $0.20/oz → B wins on unit price
Scenario 2 — Phone case:
$8 case breaks in 2 months vs. $20 case lasts 2 years
$8 × 12 = $96/year vs. $20/2 years = $10/year → $20 case wins on VALUE
Scenario 3 — Lunch:
$3 cafeteria vs. $1.50 home-packed
Is it always about price? Consider time, taste, nutrition, convenience.
Debrief: Did anyone change their mind about what "best" means? What factors besides price matter?
🎯 Opening Hook
"Raise your hand if you've ever bought something and regretted it immediately."
(Most hands go up — laugh together)
"What happened? Did you use it? Return it? Leave it in a drawer?"
"Today we learn to buy SMARTER — so fewer regrets."
✍️ Journal Prompt
Write on board:
"Describe a purchase you made that you regret. What would you do differently now?"
8 min quiet. Keep it low-stakes — even a $1 choice counts. 2 share.
