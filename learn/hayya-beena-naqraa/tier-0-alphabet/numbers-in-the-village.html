Numbers in the Village

A Hayya Beena Naqraa story · Tier 0 (Whisper of the Alphabet · هَمْسَةُ الأَبْجَدِيَّة) · For ages 3–5

A pre-reader counting book. One number, one word, one small picture per page. Read it slowly. Point to the number. Let the child count.

🗣️ Talk about it (for parent or teacher)

These aren't quiz questions. Ask one, point at the picture, and listen.

  1. Which page had the most things to count? Can we count them together, slowly?
  2. What in your room could we count? Books? Spoons? Shoes by the door?
  3. Let's say one number five times together. Which one do you like?

✏️ Try it (a tiny activity, 2 minutes)

Pick one:

  • Count fingers. Hold up your child's hand. Touch each finger and say: wāhid, ithnān, thalātha… all the way to 'ashara.
  • Go on a tiny counting walk. Find three of something. Five of something. Ten of something. Say the Arabic number out loud.
  • Stack pebbles or stones. Outside or in. Count each one as it goes on the pile: wāhid, ithnān, thalātha…

A note for grown-ups reading along

This is a pre-reader book — designed for ages 3 to 5, before children can decode numbers or letters on their own. You are the reader. Your child is the counter and the looker.

How to read it:

  1. Point to the big Arabic number at the top of the page.
  2. Say it slowly: "Wāhid. One."
  3. Point to the picture. Count out loud, touching each object: "Wāhid."
  4. On page 2, count both sandals: "Wāhid, ithnān."
  5. By page 10, you'll be counting all the way: "Wāhid, ithnān, thalātha, arba'a, khamsa, sitta, sab'a, thamāniya, tis'a, 'ashara."

Repetition is the curriculum. Read this book over and over. Within a week or two, your child will start counting along with you, then before you. That is the goal — not memorization, not testing, but the joy of numbers becoming friends.

A note on Arabic numerals: The numbers you see (١ ٢ ٣) are the original Arabic numerals — sometimes called "Eastern Arabic numerals" — still used across the Levant today. Your child will see both these and the Western forms (1, 2, 3) growing up. Both are theirs.
