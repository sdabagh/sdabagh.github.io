جِسْمِي

My Body

A Hayya Beena Naqraa story · Tier 0 (Whisper of the Alphabet · هَمْسَةُ الأَبْجَدِيَّة) · For ages 3–5

A pre-reader picture book. One body part, one tiny action, one small picture per page. Read it slowly. Point. Let the child show you the part on their own body.

🗣️ Talk about it (for parent or teacher)

These aren't quiz questions. Ask one, point at the picture, and listen.

  1. Can you show me your yad? Your anf? Your qadam? Touch each one as you say it.
  2. What does your fam like to do? Smile? Eat? Sing? Show me.
  3. Where is your qalb? Put your hand there. Can you feel it?

✏️ Try it (a tiny activity, 2 minutes)

Pick one:

  • Body part chase. The grown-up says a word in Arabic — ra's, udhun, batn — and the child touches that part as fast as they can. Switch roles.
  • Mirror game. Stand in front of a mirror together. Point to one part. Say its name in Arabic. The child copies. Move to the next.
  • Stretch and laugh. Do every action in the book in order — nod, see, hear, smile, smell, hold, walk, love, laugh, stretch. Together. That's the whole game.

A note for grown-ups reading along

This is a pre-reader book — designed for ages 3 to 5, before children can decode letters on their own. You are the reader. Your child is the listener, the looker, and the doer.

How to read it:

  1. Point to the Arabic word at the top of the page.
  2. Say it slowly: "Ra's."
  3. Touch your own head. Then gently touch theirs.
  4. Say the little action: "Uharriku ra'si. I nod my head." Nod together.
  5. That's it. Move on. Don't quiz.

This book is also a body-affirmation book. Every child's body — every size, every color, every shape, every ability — is jamīl. Beautiful. The closing page is the heart of it. Read it like you mean it, because you do.

Repetition is the curriculum. Read this book over and over. Within a week or two, your child will start touching their anf before you even say the word. That is the goal — not memorization, not testing, but the warmth of their own body becoming a friend they can name in two languages.

— Hayya Beena Naqraa (هَيَّا بِنَا نَقْرَأ)
