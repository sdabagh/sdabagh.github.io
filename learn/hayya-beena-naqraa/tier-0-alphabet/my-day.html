My Day

A Hayya Beena Naqraa story · Tier 0 (Whisper of the Alphabet · هَمْسَةُ الأَبْجَدِيَّة) · For ages 3–5

A pre-reader picture book. One letter, one word, one small picture per page. Read it slowly. Point to the letter. Let the child say the word. Then look at the picture together and remember your own day.

🗣️ Talk about it (for parent or teacher)

These aren't quiz questions. Ask one, point at the picture, and listen.

  1. Which part of your day is your favorite? Breakfast? Play? Bath? Story?
  2. What did you have for futur today? What about for 'asha'?
  3. Who tells you a qissa before sleep? Mama? Baba? Teta? Jiddo?

✏️ Try it (a tiny activity, 2 minutes)

Pick one:

  • Act out your day. Pretend to wake up, eat breakfast, go to school, play, nap, eat dinner, take a bath, hear a story, sleep. Say the Arabic word for each one as you go.
  • Find one thing in your house for one of the words: a kitab (book), a wisada (pillow), a sabun (soap), a kura (ball).
  • Look out the window tonight. Is the qamar (moon) there? Wave to it.

A note for grown-ups reading along

This is a pre-reader book — designed for ages 3 to 5, before children can decode letters on their own. You are the reader. Your child is the listener and the looker.

How to read it:

  1. Point to the big Arabic letter at the top of the page.
  2. Say the letter slowly.
  3. Point to the word and say it.
  4. Point to the picture: "Morning. Sabah."
  5. That's it. Move on. Don't quiz.

This book follows the shape of one day — your child's day. As you read, you can pause on any page and say, "This is like our morning," or "This is what Teta makes for us." The book becomes a mirror, and the words slip in through the warmth.

Repetition is the curriculum. Read this book at bedtime, at breakfast, in the car. Within a week, your child will start saying qamar before you do. That is the goal — not memorization, not testing, but the warmth of words becoming familiar friends.

A note on letter forms: Arabic letters change shape depending on where they sit in a word. For now, we only show the simple isolated form. The other forms come gently in Tier 1.
