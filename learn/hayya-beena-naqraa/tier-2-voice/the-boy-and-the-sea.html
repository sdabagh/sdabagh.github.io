The Boy and the Sea

A Hayya Beena Naqraa story · Tier 2 · For ages 7–9

Cover page

الوَلَد وَالبَحْر

The Boy and the Sea

Page 1

لَمْ يَرَ سَامِي البَحْرَ مِنْ قَبْل. اليَوْمَ، قَالَتْ مَامَا: "هَيَّا يَا حَبِيبِي. سَنَذْهَبُ إِلَى البَحْر."

Sami had never seen the sea before. Today, Mama said: "Come, habibi. We are going to the sea."

Page 2

مَشَيَا فِي شَارِعٍ صَغِير. ثُمَّ نَزَلَا دَرَجًا حَجَرِيًّا طَوِيلًا. وَفَجْأَةً، ظَهَرَ البَحْر.

They walked down a small street. Then they went down a long stone staircase. And suddenly, the sea appeared.

Page 3

كَانَ البَحْرُ أَزْرَقَ جِدًّا. أَزْرَقَ مِثْلَ السَّمَاءِ، لَكِنْ أَعْمَق. وَقَفَ سَامِي وَنَظَرَ بِعَيْنَيْنِ وَاسِعَتَيْن.

The sea was so blue. Blue like the sky, but deeper. Sami stood and looked with wide eyes.

Page 4

خَلَعَ سَامِي حِذَاءَهُ. مَشَى عَلَى الرَّمْل. كَانَ الرَّمْلُ بَارِدًا وَرَطْبًا تَحْتَ قَدَمَيْه.

Sami took off his shoes. He walked on the sand. The sand was cool and wet under his feet.

Page 5

سَمِعَ البَحْرَ يَهْمِس. شُوووش… شُوووش… كَأَنَّهُ يَقُولُ لَهُ: "أَهْلًا يَا سَامِي."

He heard the sea whisper. Shoosh… shoosh… As if it were saying to him: "Hello, Sami."

Page 6

جَاءَتْ مَوْجَةٌ صَغِيرَة. لَمَسَتْ قَدَمَيْه. قَفَزَ سَامِي مِنَ المُفَاجَأَة! ثُمَّ ضَحِكَ بِصَوْتٍ عَالٍ.

A little wave came. It touched his feet. Sami jumped from surprise! Then he laughed out loud.

Page 7

نَظَرَ سَامِي إِلَى المَاء. مَرَّتْ سَمَكَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ بِسُرْعَة. لَوَّحَ لَهَا سَامِي بِيَدِهِ وَقَالَ: "مَرْحَبًا!"

Sami looked into the water. A tiny fish darted past. Sami waved his hand and said: "Hello!"

Page 8

بَحَثَ سَامِي فِي الرَّمْل. وَجَدَ صَدَفَةً صَغِيرَة. كَانَتْ بَيْضَاءَ وَوَرْدِيَّةً، مِثْلَ أُذُنٍ صَغِيرَة.

Sami searched in the sand. He found a small shell. It was white and pink, like a tiny ear.

Page 9

بَدَأَتِ الشَّمْسُ تَنْزِل. صَارَتِ السَّمَاءُ وَرْدِيَّةً وَبُرْتُقَالِيَّة. جَلَسَ سَامِي بِجَانِبِ مَامَا وَشَاهَدَا الغُرُوبَ مَعًا.

The sun began to go down. The sky turned pink and orange. Sami sat next to Mama and they watched the sunset together.

Page 10

عَادَا إِلَى البَيْتِ بِخُطُوَاتٍ هَادِئَة. كَانَتْ يَدُ سَامِي مَقْبُوضَةً بِقُوَّة. فِي دَاخِلِهَا، الصَّدَفَةُ الصَّغِيرَة.

They walked home with quiet steps. Sami's hand was held tightly closed. Inside it, the little shell.

Page 11

فِي اللَّيْل، وَضَعَ سَامِي الصَّدَفَةَ بِجَانِبِ سَرِيرِه. أَغْمَضَ عَيْنَيْه. وَسَمِعَ البَحْرَ يَهْمِسُ مِنْ جَدِيد.

At night, Sami placed the shell next to his bed. He closed his eyes. And he heard the sea whisper again.

🗣️ Talk about it

These are not test questions — they're conversation starters. Pick one. Ask. Listen.

  1. Sami had never seen the sea before. Can you remember the first time you saw something really big — the ocean, a mountain, a tall building, snow? What did it feel like in your body?

  2. The wave surprised Sami, and then he laughed. Has something ever surprised you and then turned out to be fun? What was it?

  3. Sami picked up one small shell to take home. If you went somewhere new, what tiny thing would you pick up to remember it? Why that one?

✏️ Try it

Pick one:

  • Find a small treasure on your next walk — a pebble, a leaf, a feather, a shell. Put it somewhere you'll see it every day. Notice it for a whole week.

  • Listen like Sami listened. Sit very still for one minute, eyes closed, and count how many different sounds you can hear. Tell someone in your family what you heard.

  • Draw the sea — even if you've never seen it. Use blue, green, and white. Add one small thing that you'd want to find on the beach.

A note for grown-ups reading along

This is a Tier 2 story. The Arabic still has full vowel marks (تَشْكِيل), but the sentences are longer than Tier 1 — usually two or three short sentences per page. Your reader is starting to hold a small idea across more than one line.

Read the Arabic first, slowly. Let your child watch your finger move right-to-left. Then read the English. On the second reading, ask your child to read along with you on the words they recognize — baḥr, raml, azraq. By the third time, they may be reading the short lines on their own.

If your family has been to the Mediterranean — Beirut, Tripoli, Tyre, Latakia, Jaffa, Gaza, anywhere on that long beautiful coast — tell your child your own first-sea story. The book is a doorway. Your story is the room.

— Hayya Beena Naqraa (هَيَّا بِنَا نَقْرَأ)
