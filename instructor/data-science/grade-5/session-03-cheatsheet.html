Materials Needed
1 opaque jar or bag per pair (100 beads total — 4 colors) True distribution key (kept hidden from students until debrief) Worksheets (1 per student) Pencils & colored pencils for graphing Small cups for drawing beads
Jar Setup — True Distribution:
40 red · 30 blue · 20 yellow · 10 green = 100 total
(40% / 30% / 20% / 10%)
Pre-count and prep 1 jar per pair. Keep the true % secret until debrief. Beads available at craft stores; colored counters or M&Ms work too.
Key Vocabulary
Population — the entire group you want to learn about
Sample — a smaller group selected from the population to represent it
Random sample — every member of the population has an equal chance of selection
Convenience sample — you pick whoever is easiest to reach (introduces bias)
Stratified sample — population is divided into groups; you sample from each group proportionally
Representative — a sample that reflects the true proportions of the population
Sample size — the number of individuals in the sample
Percentage — part ÷ whole × 100; used to compare samples of different sizes
Discussion Questions + Teacher Notes
  • "Why can't we just ask every single person in a population?"
    → Time, cost, access. A national survey of 330 million people is impractical. A good sample of 1,000 can represent the whole accurately if chosen carefully. This is how polls, census estimates, and medical trials work.
  • "What's wrong with a convenience sample?"
    → It's biased toward whoever is easiest to reach — often friends, nearby people, or people who volunteer. Example: asking only your friends what music is popular gives you a biased picture of your whole grade.
  • "After your 10-bead draw, was your sample representative?"
    → Probably not perfectly. That's the point — small samples are noisier. After the 30-bead draw, most pairs will be closer to the true distribution. The experience IS the lesson.
  • "If you drew 50 beads, would it definitely be more accurate than 30?"
    → More likely, but not guaranteed. Probability means you can still get a bad sample by chance. Larger sample = better odds, not a certainty. This previews the Law of Large Numbers (S5).
  • "How do you know when your sample is big enough?"
    → It depends on how varied the population is, and how precise you need to be. Real statisticians use formulas; for now, the rule of thumb is: larger samples are more reliable, and a sample should be at least 10% of the population for small groups.
Bead Simulation — Full Setup Guide
Pairs work together. Each pair gets 1 jar. True distribution is hidden. Students draw without looking.
Steps:
  1. Without looking, draw 10 beads. Record color counts. Put beads back. Shake jar.
  2. Convert to percentages: count ÷ 10 × 100. Record on worksheet.
  3. Without looking, draw 30 beads. Record. Convert to percentages: count ÷ 30 × 100.
  4. Reveal the true distribution (40/30/20/10%). Students compare their two samples.
  5. Class discussion: which sample was closer? Did anyone get a very wrong result with 10 draws?
Key debrief question: "Which sample was closer to the true distribution — your 10-bead or 30-bead draw? Why do you think that happened?"
Expected result: Most pairs' 30-bead samples will be closer to the true distribution. Some pairs will have surprising 10-bead results — use these as examples of why sample size matters.
Opening Hook
Hold up the jar of 100 beads. Ask: "Without counting every single bead — how could you figure out approximately how many of each color there are?"
Take one student guess. Then: "That's exactly what sampling is. Let's see how well it works."
→ Don't explain sampling yet. Let the curiosity about the jar drive the lesson forward.
Analysis Writing Prompt
Write on board:
"Which sample size gave you a more accurate picture of the jar — 10 beads or 30 beads? Use your data to explain. What would happen with 50 beads? 90?"
5 min writing. Students should cite their actual numbers, not just say "30 was better."
Strong response: "My 10-bead draw showed 50% red but the true amount was 40%. My 30-bead draw showed 43% red — much closer. Larger samples reduce the effect of chance."
ND-Friendly Tips
  • Tactile is the lesson — The physical act of drawing beads without looking IS the concept of random sampling. Don't rush to abstract definitions.
  • Focus on the "why" first — Before any vocabulary, establish: "We can't count everything, so we take a sample. The question is: how do we make it fair?"
  • Percentage calculation scaffold — Some students may struggle with the math. Write on board: count ÷ total × 100 = %. Walk through one example together before they work independently.
  • Allow retesting — Students who want to draw a third sample should be encouraged. Curiosity about what happens with even more draws is exactly the right instinct.
  • Normalize surprising results — If a pair gets wildly wrong results with 10 draws, celebrate it: "This is exactly why sample size matters — and why one bad sample can mislead us!"
