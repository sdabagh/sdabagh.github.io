📚 Key Vocabulary
Hypothesis — a testable prediction about what you expect to find
Testable — can be investigated with real data; not a matter of opinion
Variable — something that can change or differ between subjects
Prediction — a specific, measurable expected outcome
Expected result — what you think the data will show before you collect it
Null hypothesis — the default position: "I expect no difference/relationship"
HYPOTHESIS FORMULA CARD (print & distribute)
"I think ___ because ___."
"I predict that ___."
A hypothesis is an educated guess — not a commitment. It's OK if you're wrong!
💬 Discussion Questions + Teacher Notes
  • "Is 'Why do people like pizza?' a research question?"
    → No — it's too vague and can't be measured. Help students see: a research question must be specific enough that you could design a study to answer it. "Do 5th graders prefer pizza or pasta in our cafeteria?" is better.
  • "What's the difference between a hypothesis and a guess?"
    → A hypothesis is an educated guess — grounded in something you already know or observed. "I think kids prefer pizza because our school sells more of it" uses evidence. A random guess doesn't.
  • "Can your hypothesis be wrong?"
    → YES — and that's fine! This is critical. The goal of a hypothesis is to give you a direction to test. If data disproves it, you learned something. Frame "being wrong" as success in science.
  • "Why do scientists write their prediction BEFORE collecting data?"
    → To avoid unconsciously looking for data that confirms what they already believe. Pre-registration is a real scientific practice. This is a preview of confirmation bias (S4).
  • "What is the null hypothesis? Why does it matter?"
    → Simple version: "I expect no difference." It's the skeptic's starting position. Scientists have to show their findings are real, not random. Analogy: "innocent until proven guilty."
🔬 Question-Sharpening Activity — Setup
Display or print these 5 vague questions. Students work in pairs to sharpen each into a testable hypothesis using the Formula Card. 20 min work + 5 min share.
The 5 Vague Questions:
  1. "Do kids sleep enough?" → sharpen to: who, how much, compared to what?
  2. "Does music help you study?" → sharpen to: what type? what subject? how measured?
  3. "Are phones bad for kids?" → sharpen to: bad how? which outcome? which kids?
  4. "Do people eat healthier now?" → sharpen to: now vs. when? which people? what counts as healthy?
  5. "Is exercise good for grades?" → sharpen to: how much exercise? which grades? over what time?
Example strong hypothesis: "I think 5th graders who exercise at least 30 min/day will have higher math quiz averages than those who don't, because exercise increases focus."
Watch for: Students who write opinions instead of predictions. Redirect: "Could you collect data to test that? Would you and a friend agree on the measurement?"
🎯 Opening Hook
Write these 3 questions on the board:
"Why do people like music?" / "Are kids healthy?" / "Is homework useful?"
Ask: "If I gave you 1 week to collect data to answer one of these — could you? What would stop you?"
→ Too vague, too big, too opinion-based. Help students see the gap between interesting questions and answerable ones.
✍️ Independent Hypothesis Write
Write on board:
"Think of ONE topic you're curious about. Write a hypothesis using the Formula Card. Include: what you think, why, and what you predict."
6 min quiet writing. Circulate and check: Does it name a specific measurable outcome? Could data confirm or deny it?
Strong example: "I think students who eat breakfast perform better on morning quizzes because their brains have more energy. I predict breakfast-eaters will score 10+ points higher on average."
🧠 ND-Friendly Tips
  • Hypothesis card = anchor — The formula card removes the blank-page paralysis. Keep it visible all session. Students who feel stuck should return to the card first.
  • Frame as educated guess, not commitment — Say repeatedly: "Your hypothesis can be wrong. Wrong hypotheses are scientifically valuable. You haven't failed if data disagrees."
  • Partner work before solo — Sharpening questions is hard alone. Pair students before asking for independent hypotheses.
  • Topic choice matters — Students who choose topics they care about write significantly better hypotheses. Allow wide topic latitude for the independent write.
  • Null hypothesis: keep it simple — Introduce as "the I-see-no-difference position." Don't require formal notation. The concept is the goal.
